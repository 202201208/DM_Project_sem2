Noise Reduction

In image processing, noise refers to unwanted random variations or distortions that are introduced into an image during its acquisition, transmission, or processing. These variations can degrade the quality of the image and affect its visual appearance and analysis.

Noise is typically considered as an additive or multiplicative disturbance that alters the pixel values in the image.

DIFFERENT TYPES OF NOISE :

1. GAUSSIAN NOISE :

Gaussian Noise is a statical additive noise that follows a Gaussian Distribution. Random Gaussian function is added to Image function to generate this noise. It is also called as electronic noise because it is often cause by electronic components due to poor illumination or high temperature.

2. Salt and Pepper Noise :

Salt and Pepper Noise is also known as impulse noise, it appears as randomly occurring white and black pixels in an image, resembling grains of salt and pepper. Salt and pepper noise is typically caused by errors in data transmission or faulty image sensors.

3. Poisson Noise :

The appearance of this noise is seen due to the statistical nature of electromagnetic waves such as x-rays, visible lights and gamma rays. It is common in low-light conditions and affects images captured with low exposure times. Poisson noise appears as random variations in brightness and can be particularly important in medical imaging

MEDIAN FILTER :

The median filter is a popular technique used in digital image processing for noise reduction and preserving edges.

The median filter operates by replacing each pixel value with the median value of its neighboring pixels within a specified window. The median value is the middle value when the pixel values are sorted in ascending or descending order.

The choice of the filter/window size is crucial in the performance of the median filter. A small window size may not adequately reduce noise, while a large window size can blur the image

MEAN FILTER :

Mean filter is the used to reduce the intensities in an image. It uses convolution mask to process. The window has some specific size which calculates the mean by considering the neighborhood pixels.

It operates by replacing each pixel value with the average value of its neighboring pixels within a specified window

The filter is particularly useful for reducing Gaussian noise. The mean filter tends to produce more blurring compared to the Gaussian filter. The choice of the window size is crucial in the performance of the mean filter. A smaller window size may not adequately reduce noise, while a larger window size can lead to more blurring.

Advantage of the mean filter is its simplicity and computational efficiency. It is straightforward to implement and can be applied quickly to large images.

Some of it’s limitations are that the mean filter may not effectively reduce impulsive noise, such as salt-and-pepper noise, as it treats all pixel values equally and it can cause blurring and loss of fine details, especially if the window size is large.

MIN FILTER :

Min Filter operates by replacing each pixel value with the minimum value of its neighboring pixels within a specified window.

It is particularly useful for reducing impulsive noise, such as salt-and-pepper noise, where random pixels are corrupted with very high or very low intensity values.

Advantage of the min filter is its ability to preserve sharp edges and fine details in the image. Drawback of this filter is that it can cause darkening or loss of details in areas where noise is present but not desired to be reduced. Additionally, the filter can amplify noise in regions with low-intensity values.

MAX FILTER :

Max Filter operates by replacing each pixel value with the maximum value of its neighboring pixels within a specified window.

It is particularly useful for reducing impulsive noise, such as salt-and-pepper noise, where random pixels are corrupted with very high or very low intensity values.

Advantage of the max filter is its ability to preserve sharp edges and fine details in the image.

Drawback of this filter is that it can cause brightening or loss of details in areas where noise is present but not desired to be reduced. Additionally, the filter can amplify noise in regions with high-intensity values.
